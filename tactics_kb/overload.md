# Overload

An **overload** is a deliberate local numerical advantage — putting more players in one zone of the pitch than the opponent has defenders there — to make the opposition choose between two bad options. Classic example: a full-back, a winger, and an attacking midfielder combining in the right half-space against a single full-back. The defender either steps and leaves a runner free, or holds and the attacker gets time to pick a pass.

Overloads are usually engineered on one side to force the opponent to shift across, then exploited by switching play *to the underload* — the now-vacated weak side — with a quick diagonal pass or a switch. "Overload to isolate" is the same idea applied to leaving a 1-v-1 specialist (a winger) free on the far touchline.

Signals in the data:
- A cluster of completed passes in a small zone (often a half-space) involving three or more attackers.
- A switch of play (a long sideways pass crossing the central third) immediately following the cluster.
- The next event after the switch is often a 1-v-1 carry or a cross into the box.

Related concepts: third-man runs (the unmarked third player benefiting from a 1-2 between two others), rotations (positional swaps that create temporary numerical advantage).
